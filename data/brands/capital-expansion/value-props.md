# Capital Expansion — value props (the four pillars)

These are the load-bearing claims. The copywriter weaves these into per-recipient creative; the voice agent reinforces them on inbound calls.

## 1. No origination conflicts

We are not a lender. We do not earn a spread. Our incentive is matching, full stop.

- "We don't make money on the spread."
- "We have no incentive to push a more expensive product."
- "Our only product is the match."

Why this matters to the operator: most "matchmakers" are brokers earning a percentage of the originated facility. That tilts every recommendation toward the most expensive product the operator will accept. We don't have that gravity.

## 2. Operator-first

Every match starts with the operator's situation, not a partner's quota.

- "If we don't have a fit, we say so."
- "We don't push products to fill a partner's pipeline."
- "Your situation is the input. The match is the output. Nothing in between."

Why this matters: most marketplaces optimize for partner conversion, which means the operator gets routed to whichever partner is cheapest to acquire that week.

## 3. Across capital types

We are not stuck pitching one product. We see the situation and route to the right tool — factoring, SBA, equipment finance, ABL, RBF, or LOC.

- "We're product-agnostic."
- "Most operators ask for a line of credit. Sometimes that's right. Often it isn't. We tell you which."
- "Six capital types under one roof. We pick the right one before you talk to anyone."

Why this matters: a single-product shop will always recommend their product. An operator with the wrong product spends six months unwinding it.

## 4. Warm intros, not cold lists

When we connect an operator to a partner, the conversation is already framed.

- "Your situation is in the partner's inbox before the call starts."
- "No starting from zero."
- "No reciting your story for the fourth time this week."
- "Walk into a conversation that already knows who you are."

Why this matters: capital conversations have huge time-cost for operators. A warm intro with context drops the time-to-yes-or-no from weeks to days.

## How the four pillars compose

The four pillars are designed to compose into a single argument:

> Capital Expansion is what a capital marketplace would look like if (a) the matchmaker didn't earn on the spread, (b) the operator's situation was the only input, (c) every capital type was available, and (d) the partner already had context before the first call.

That's the brand. Copywriters can quote that whole composition or any single pillar.

## What we explicitly position against

- **Lead-gen marketplaces** (we contrast: "private network, not a lead-gen farm")
- **Single-product shops** (we contrast: "six capital types, not one")
- **Brokers earning on origination** (we contrast: "no origination conflicts")
- **Cold lists / cold outreach to lenders** (we contrast: "warm intros, not cold lists")
