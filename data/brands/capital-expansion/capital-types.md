# Capital Expansion — capital types

Six capital types Capital Expansion routes operators across. Each has a one-line definition, the canonical use case, the wrong-fit flag, and the typical demand-side partner archetype.

## Factoring

**Definition.** Sell outstanding invoices to a factoring partner; get paid immediately instead of waiting 30–90 days for the customer to pay.

**Right fit:** Net-30 to net-90 commercial AR; B2B; recurring customers; operator's working capital is locked in receivables.

**Wrong fit:** Mostly consumer/retail revenue; collections-heavy or disputed invoices; customer concentration so high one default kills the program.

**Common for:** Freight & trucking, manufacturing, staffing & PEO, wholesale distribution, government contractors.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** specialty factors per vertical (transportation factors, staffing factors, USG-AR factors, general SMB factors).

**Operator language:** "I can't wait 60 days to get paid; I have payroll Friday."

## SBA Loans

**Definition.** Government-backed term loans (most commonly 7(a) and 504) for business acquisition, owner-occupied commercial real estate, or long-term working capital. Personal guarantee required; typically 10-year amortization on working capital, longer on real estate.

**Right fit:** Acquisitions, owner-occupied CRE, partner buyouts, long-horizon expansion. Operator with strong personal credit, 2+ years of profitability, willing to PG.

**Wrong fit:** Bridging short-term cash flow; fast-close needs (SBA closings are slow); operators with weak personal credit or unwilling to PG.

**Common for:** Acquisitions, owner-occupied CRE, dental/medical/veterinary practice buy-ins, restaurant build-outs, manufacturing facility buys.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** SBA-preferred lender banks, non-bank SBA specialists.

**Operator language:** "I'm buying out my partner / buying the building / acquiring a competitor."

## Equipment Finance

**Definition.** Loan or lease against new or existing equipment; sale-leaseback unlocks cash from owned equipment without diluting equity.

**Right fit:** Hard-asset purchases (trucks, trailers, machine tools, kitchen, dental chairs, imaging, CNCs). Operator with collateral-quality equipment.

**Wrong fit:** Software, consumables, soft costs.

**Common for:** Construction, manufacturing, trucking, healthcare practices, restaurants.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** equipment finance specialists per asset class (transportation, machine tools, medical, restaurant).

**Operator language:** "I need a new tractor / a new CNC / new chairs / new ovens, and the dealer wants 25% down."

## Asset-Based Lending (ABL)

**Definition.** Revolving line of credit secured by accounts receivable, inventory, machinery, and equipment. Borrowing base recalculated regularly against eligible collateral.

**Right fit:** Operators with substantive AR + inventory; revenue $5M+; working capital needs that flex with the seasonal/contract cycle.

**Wrong fit:** Pure-services businesses with no inventory and small AR; operators who need a one-time term loan rather than a revolver.

**Common for:** Wholesale, distribution, retail, manufacturing.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** non-bank ABL shops, regional bank ABL groups.

**Operator language:** "My banker said I'm too big for a small line and not ready for an ABL. I think I'm actually ready for an ABL."

## Lines of Credit

**Definition.** Revolving credit, unsecured or lightly-secured, smaller than an ABL. Used to smooth short-term cash-flow gaps.

**Right fit:** Profitable operator, modest working capital needs ($50K–$500K), wants flexibility without putting up specific collateral.

**Wrong fit:** Operators trying to fund a one-time large purchase (use term loan or equipment finance instead).

**Common for:** Professional services, e-commerce, restaurants, healthcare practices.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** community banks, non-bank fintech LOC providers, business credit cards as adjacent product.

**Operator language:** "I want a cushion for slow months."

## Revenue-Based Financing (RBF)

**Definition.** Non-dilutive growth capital repaid as a percentage of future sales. No fixed amortization; the repayment scales with the business.

**Right fit:** SaaS, e-commerce, healthcare, subscription businesses with predictable recurring revenue. Operator wants to avoid equity dilution; growth-stage.

**Wrong fit:** Lumpy revenue; one-time-purchase businesses; operators whose unit economics can't absorb a 5–8% revenue share for 12–24 months.

**Common for:** SaaS, e-commerce, healthcare, subscription/recurring businesses.

**Demand-side partner archetypes:** RBF specialists (per vertical and stage).

**Operator language:** "I want growth capital but not a VC round."

## How to use this file in creative

When authoring per-recipient creative, route the operator to ONE capital type as the primary recommendation in the piece. Mention a second only if it's a near-miss adjacent fit.

Do NOT recommend three options in the same piece. The brand position is "we know who funds what" — three options reads like a marketplace, which contradicts the brand.
