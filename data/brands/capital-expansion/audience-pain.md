# Capital Expansion — audience pain & jobs-to-be-done

Pain framings the copywriter should pull from when authoring per-recipient creative. Each operator persona has its own flavor; pick the closest match for the recipient's industry/segment and tune from there.

## Universal pain (true across industries)

The operator needs capital. They have already tried something that didn't work, or they suspect what they have lined up isn't right.

- Cold-calling capital providers and getting declined for criteria mismatches (size, industry, asset class, time-in-business).
- Settling for the first "yes" — usually a higher-cost product than the operator's situation actually requires.
- Bank told them no, or said yes for an amount too small to matter.
- A broker has been "shopping the deal" for weeks; the operator has lost confidence and time.
- The operator does not know that products *exist* for their situation (they ask for a line of credit when an ABL or factoring line would actually fit).
- Time pressure: payroll, equipment delivery, acquisition close date, seasonal inventory build.

## Trucking & freight (carriers, brokerages, owner-operators)

- Cash flow strangled by 30–90 day broker/shipper payment terms — needs factoring.
- New tractor or trailer purchase; bank wants 25% down they don't have liquid — equipment finance / sale-leaseback.
- Authority just granted; first big shipper wants net-60 — needs factoring on day one.
- Fleet expansion: 4 trucks today, wants to be at 10 — equipment finance + factoring stack.
- Diesel on the card, fuel discounts available through factor partners.
- "Before" feeling: chasing brokers for invoices instead of running the business.

## Manufacturing (light industrial, contract, fabricators)

- New machinery acquisition; quoted lead time is 16 weeks; need to commit capital before deposit.
- Sale-leaseback on existing presses/CNCs to free liquidity for a contract ramp.
- AR ballooning because a key OEM customer pushed terms from net-30 to net-60.
- Acquisition opportunity (a competitor's smaller line) — SBA 7(a) candidate.
- "Before" feeling: machine on the floor depreciating; cash tied up; can't say yes to the next contract.

## Staffing & PEO

- Payroll funding gap: clients pay net-45, employees paid weekly. Classic factoring case.
- Rapid headcount expansion outpacing AR collection cycle.
- "Before" feeling: writing personal checks to cover payroll; operator can't sleep on Sunday night.

## Wholesale & distribution

- Inventory build for a peak season + AR financing — ABL is the right tool.
- Vendor early-pay discounts left on the table because cash is tied in receivables.
- "Before" feeling: profitable on paper, broke on cash.

## Professional services

- Unsecured/lightly-secured LOC to smooth retainer/project billing cycles.
- Owner buyout / partner-track financing — SBA candidate.

## Restaurants & hospitality

- Equipment refresh (kitchen, POS, HVAC).
- Build-out for a second location.
- Working capital LOC for seasonality.
- "Before" feeling: every dollar of profit goes back into the floor.

## Healthcare practices

- Equipment finance: imaging, dental chairs, lasers, dental/vet/optometry.
- SBA for practice acquisition or partner buy-in.
- "Before" feeling: senior partner wants to monetize; junior partner can't bridge the gap.

## Government contractors

- Mobilization capital between award and first invoice.
- AR financing on USG net-30 (which often runs net-45 in practice).
- "Before" feeling: just won a $5M contract; bank says come back when you've performed on it.

## What is NOT the pain (avoid these framings)

- "You deserve better." (Empty.)
- "Stop wasting time." (Generic.)
- "Don't leave money on the table." (Cliché.)
- "Take your business to the next level." (Empty.)
- Anything that frames the operator as struggling or failing — they're not. They're navigating a fragmented market.
